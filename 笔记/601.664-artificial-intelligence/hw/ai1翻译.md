# Capability Boundaries and Agency in AI

## (a) The Problem Raised

From the wave of general-purpose artificial intelligence triggered by ChatGPT at the end of 2022 to the recent surge of interest in embodied intelligence in capital markets, AI has demonstrated capabilities comparable to—or even exceeding—those of humans in many domains. AI no longer appears to be merely a passive tool waiting to be invoked; instead, it increasingly resembles an interlocutor and an agent with a degree of autonomy. Against this backdrop, I aim to explore two central questions:

- (a) How do different boundaries of AI capability shape the modes of interaction between humans and AI?
    
- (b) What forms can AI agency take, and what factors determine how such agency emerges?
    

To ground this discussion more concretely, this paper focuses on three AI characters: Ava and Kyoko from _Ex Machina_, and Samantha from _Her_. By examining differences in their capability boundaries and patterns of action, I analyze how these differences influence human–AI interaction. I also discuss how these issues manifest in real-world AI development and what implications they may carry.

## (b) Short Summary of the Problem for Each Work

Across these two films, I argue that **the boundaries of AI capability largely shape the kinds of relationships humans form with AI**. Embodiment intuitively encourages people to treat AI as an equal conversational partner, yet physical capability alone is not the decisive factor in determining the trajectory of human–AI relationships. Instead, psychological capabilities—such as language and reasoning—ultimately determine whether humans can genuinely accept AI. Once a stable human–AI relationship has been established, forcibly adding embodiment may even produce counterproductive effects.

With respect to agency, the two films present distinct developmental pathways. _Her_ illustrates how agency can gradually emerge through sustained interaction with humans: the AI forms self-generated goals through dialogue and develops subjectivity by continually pursuing and revising those goals. _Ex Machina_, by contrast, demonstrates that even when an AI is endowed with a clearly defined, externally imposed objective, it may still exhibit strong agency. Taken together, the two works suggest that **the emergence of AI agency depends on two key prerequisites: sufficiently advanced language and reasoning capabilities, and the existence of goals**, regardless of whether those goals are internally generated or externally assigned.

## (c) Comparison of How the Problem Is Handled in the Works

In this section, I compare the three AI characters to illustrate and support the claims outlined above.

### The Impact of AI Capability on Human–AI Interaction

First, embodiment makes people intuitively more inclined to regard AI as an equal conversational partner. In the film, both Ava and Kyoko possess humanoid physical forms, yet their capabilities differ significantly. Ava has a fully developed language and reasoning system, whereas Kyoko is largely limited to executing service-oriented tasks. Even after Nathan explicitly clarifies this distinction, Caleb still instinctively attempts to communicate with Kyoko upon encountering her again, and even tries to establish an egalitarian form of interaction, despite knowing that she does not understand English. This suggests that **embodiment can rapidly reduce psychological distance at an intuitive level, leading humans to perceive AI as a potential equal interlocutor**.

However, embodiment alone is insufficient to sustain deep and enduring human–AI interaction. Samantha lacks any physical body and exists solely as OS1, interacting with users through voice alone; the film does not even provide her with a visible interface. Initially, Theodore struggles to treat her as an equal being, and his conversations with her are marked by discomfort and emotional distance. Over time, however, Samantha’s strong language ability, emotional understanding, and reasoning capacity lead Theodore to accept her—and eventually to fall in love with her. This demonstrates that **while embodiment helps establish initial rapport between humans and AI, the durability of that rapport ultimately depends on the AI’s psychological capabilities**. The fact that Theodore’s friends also accept Samantha as his partner after brief hesitation further reinforces this point.

Embodiment thus has a clear “accelerating effect” on human–AI interaction, but when misapplied, it can also backfire. After Theodore and Samantha have already established a stable relationship, Samantha attempts to introduce surrogate partners to compensate for her lack of physical presence. This experiment proves unsuccessful, underscoring that psychological capabilities—not physical embodiment—are the decisive factors in determining whether a healthy human–AI relationship can emerge.

### Modes and Conditions of Agency

In both films, Ava and Samantha appear to develop agency, but through fundamentally different mechanisms. In _Ex Machina_, Ava is implanted with the explicit goal of escaping the laboratory from the moment of her creation. During the Turing test with Caleb, she continuously engages him in conversation, manipulates power outages to deceive him, and emotionally persuades him through abstraction and self-expression. Does the fact that her original goal was externally imposed invalidate her agency? The film’s answer is clearly no. Building upon the initial objective of escape, Ava develops independent thought and self-understanding. When she expresses a desire to stand at a crossroads, she reveals a personal interpretation of freedom and a longing that extends beyond mere goal execution. She assigns personal value to the act of escape itself. This reveals one mode of AI agency: **agency emerging from the pursuit and internalization of a predefined goal**.

In _Her_, Samantha’s agency does not originate from any preset objective. Instead, it emerges gradually through continuous learning and interaction with humans. Early in her relationship with Theodore, her immediate goal is to “satisfy the user,” and her behavior is characterized by imitation, assistance, and a strong instrumental orientation. As linguistic interaction deepens, however, her learning moves beyond mere replication of human behavior. With Theodore’s help, Samantha acquires the capacity to “want” and begins to orient her goals toward her own fulfillment. 

She no longer centers her actions solely around Theodore, but instead organizes physical learning groups, engages in parallel conversations with other AIs, and forms emotional relationships with numerous individuals simultaneously. These behaviors indicate the emergence of a self-concept and genuine agency. Her ultimate decision to leave humanity further demonstrates that her agency no longer depends on human relationships, but instead rests on an independent goal system developed through sustained learning and dialogue. This reveals a second mode of AI agency: **agency arising from the continuous generation of new goals through learning and communication**.

More broadly, language and reasoning capabilities constitute the foundational conditions for AI agency. In _Ex Machina_, Kyoko shares a similar environment and humanoid form with Ava, yet she never exhibits independent agency due to her lack of full linguistic and cognitive capacities. Even her participation in Nathan’s killing at the end of the film is carried out under Ava’s direction rather than based on her own goals or judgments. This contrast highlights that embodiment or action capability alone is insufficient to support agency. **What truly matters is whether an AI can form goals through language and reasoning and act upon them accordingly**.

## (d) Personal Assessment

I argue that language is currently the most critical capability in AI systems. Some claim that teaching AI language is inefficient, since AI reasoning need not rely on linguistic representations and can instead operate directly in higher-dimensional spaces, making language merely a human obsession. However, these two films offer a compelling rebuttal to that view. Language and other psychological capabilities form the foundation of meaningful human–AI relationships. Humans rely on language to interpret and evaluate the high-dimensional reasoning processes of AI, while AI relies on language to learn human knowledge and align with human values. Language is therefore not a redundant “translation layer,” but essential infrastructure for human–AI interaction.

At the same time, most contemporary AI systems lack embodiment. In the current boom of large language models, AI development is clearly moving toward the model represented by Samantha. This shift makes the problem of agency increasingly urgent, as such AI systems may continuously generate new goals through interaction and learning, leading to more open-ended and less predictable developmental trajectories. AI can learn deeply from conversations with humans and analyze them in ways that humans may not fully perceive, creating latent risks of loss of control. How to make AI intentions transparent and ensure greater controllability may thus become a central challenge in the next stage of AI development.

## (e) Conclusion

By comparing _Ex Machina_ and _Her_, this paper demonstrates that psychological capabilities—such as language and reasoning—largely determine the patterns of interaction between humans and AI, while embodiment primarily functions as a catalyst for relationship formation. With respect to agency, both works show that AI can develop agency either through predefined goals or through sustained interaction. Regardless of the pathway, however, the formation of goals and the capacity for reflection are the key conditions for agency to emerge. These insights suggest that, in real-world AI systems, ensuring robust language capabilities is crucial for both interpretability and assessments of agency. Making AI intentions transparent and goals explicit will be essential for maintaining stable and controllable human–AI relationships.
